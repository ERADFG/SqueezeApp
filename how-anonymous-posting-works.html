A Short History of Anonymous Text Boards

Anonymous text boards aren't a new idea — they're one of the oldest formats on the internet, and understanding where they came from explains why they still work today.

Bulletin board systems

Long before the modern web, dial-up bulletin board systems (BBSes) in the 1980s let users post messages to shared boards, often under handles rather than verified identities. These systems established the basic pattern: a shared space, threaded messages, and low barriers to posting.

Usenet and early forums

Usenet newsgroups extended that idea across the internet in the 1980s and 90s, organizing discussion into topic-based threads that anyone could reply to without a centralized account system in the early years.

Imageboards popularize true anonymity

In the 2000s, imageboard-style platforms popularized the format most people associate with "anonymous posting" today: no required username, an optional tripcode for continuity within a thread, and content ranked by engagement rather than follower count.

The shift toward profile-based social media

As mainstream social platforms grew, most shifted toward persistent profiles, follower counts, and algorithmic feeds tied to identity. That made anonymous boards feel increasingly like an alternative rather than the default.
